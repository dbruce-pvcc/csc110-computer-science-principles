Mission

The U.S. Department of the Interior manages and protects natural resources and cultural heritage. It provides scientific information about the natural resources in the United States. It has the responsibility and special commitment to honor American Indians, Alaska Natives, and affiliated Island Communities.

Priorities

Work towards addressing four intersecting challenges including COVID-19, economic recovery, racial equity, and climate change as mandated by President Biden. To meet the challenges, the department is focusing on the following:

Responsibly develop renewable energy on public lands and waters.

  • The department is investing in climate research and environmental innovation for clean energy solutions with the goal to restore balance on public lands and waters to benefit future generations.

Strengthen relationships with sovereign Tribal Nations.

  • Recognize the importance of honoring federal trust responsibility and allowing sovereignty and self-governance of Tribal Nations.

Work to conserve at least 30% each of our lands and waters by the year 2030.

  • Protect biodiversity, slow extinction rates, and help leverage natural climate solutions. This will require support for local, state, private, and Tribal nature conservation and restoration efforts across America.

Center equity and environmental justice across the United States.

  • Work to correct multiple crises impacts across the Nation and recognize these impacts are not evenly distributed in our society. Communities of color, low-income, rural, and Indigenous communities suffer disproportionate and cumulative harm from environmental pollution.

Strategic Goals 2022-2026

    Goal 1:

    Promote well-being, equity, and justice for Tribes, American Indians, Alaska Natives, Native Hawaiians, and Insular Communities.

    • Promote effective government relationships between the U.S. Department of the Interior and Tribes and ensure treaty obligations and trust are upheld.
    • Ensure tribes are safe and healthy, have economic security and growth, and are provided with the education necessary for future Success.

    Goal 2:

    Protect, manage, restore, and conserve natural and cultural resources from climate change and other environmental stressors.

    • Conserve lands, water, and heritage for future generations.
    • Maintain healthy, protected, and sustained species, habitats, and ecosystems.
    • Promote pride and enjoyment in our lands and heritage.

    Goal 3:

    Balance the use of natural resources in a sustainable manner while supporting communities and the economy.

    • Promote meeting current and future energy needs through renewable energy and sustainable sources.
    • Ensure land, water and non-energy mineral resources support the diverse needs of communities.

    Goal 4:

    Serve and honor the public trust.

    • Ensure all people, communities, and organizations are benefited from data, science, and information from the U.S. Department of the Interior.
    • Provide equitable and just access to opportunities, services, and resources.
    • Ensure the U.S. Department of the Interior workforce is diverse, safe, engaged, and committed to the Mission.
    • Maintain confidence and satisfaction in the U.S. Department of the Interior.

History


"Department of the Interior Seal" by DonkeyHotey is licensed under CC BY 2.0.

The Department of the Interior was created from a bill that passed on March 3, 1849, and charged with taking care of the Nation's internal affairs. The department has often been referred to as the Department of Everything since it has had many government responsibilities. The original focus of the department was internal development of the Nation and the welfare of its people. Many of the original domestic concerns the department dealt with were gradually transferred to other departments. For example, the Department of Interior was responsible for water pollution control prior to the creation of the Environmental Protection Agency. Another agency that later became a separate department was the Department of Agriculture. However, protection and management of natural resources, Indigenous tribal concerns, and wildlife conservation have remained the responsibility of the Department of the Interior.

In 2001 Gale A. Norton was nominated and become the first woman to serve as Secretary of the Interior. The current secretary is Deb Haaland and she made history by being the first Native American to serve as cabinet secretary. She is a member of the Pueblo of Laguna and 35th generation New Mexican. In 2014, she became the first Native American woman to be elected to lead a State Party by becoming the New Mexico Lieutenant Governor. She is also one of the first Native American women to serve in Congress focusing on environmental justice, climate change, missing and murdered indigenous women, and family policies.


Responsibilities

The Department of the Interior has a central role in how the United States manages public lands, protects the environment, ensures environmental justice, and honors relationships with indigenous communities. The U.S. Department of the Interior manages America's natural and cultural resources. It is a Cabinet-level agency that employs about 70,000 people. This includes eleven technical bureaus made up of scientists and resource management professionals.

  • Bureau of Indian Affairs
  • Bureau of Indian Education
  • Bureau of Land Management
  • Bureau of Ocean Energy Management
  • Bureau of Reclamation
  • Bureau of Safety and Environmental Enforcement
  • Bureau of Trust Funds Administration
  • National Park Service
  • Office of Surface Mining Reclamation and Enforcement
  • U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service
  • U.S. Geological Survey
